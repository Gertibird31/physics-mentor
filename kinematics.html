Big Idea 1: Position, Displacement, and Motion Graphs

Goal: describe motion in words, equations, and graphs.

Articles

  • Reference frames and coordinate choices
  • Distance vs displacement
  • Interpreting position-time and velocity-time graphs

Reference frames and coordinate choices

Every motion statement needs an origin and a positive direction. Switching coordinate systems changes signs, not physics.

Distance vs displacement

Distance is total path length (scalar). Displacement is final position minus initial position (vector-like in 1D with sign).

Motion graphs

On x-t graphs, slope is velocity. On v-t graphs, slope is acceleration and signed area gives displacement.

Big Idea 2: 1D Constant-Acceleration Kinematics

Goal: solve linear motion problems using kinematic equations and units.

Articles

  • Average vs instantaneous velocity/acceleration
  • Choosing the right kinematic equation
  • Sign conventions and common mistakes

Average vs instantaneous quantities

Average values describe change over intervals; instantaneous values are the local value at a moment in time.

Kinematic equation selection

List known variables among x, v, v₀, a, t first. Then choose the equation containing the unknown but not extra unknowns.

Signs and direction

Negative acceleration does not always mean slowing down; it means acceleration points in the negative coordinate direction.

Big Idea 3: 2D Projectile Motion

Goal: split vectors into components and recombine for full projectile solutions.

Articles

  • Velocity components at launch
  • Independence of horizontal and vertical motion
  • Time of flight, peak height, and range

Launch components

Resolve initial velocity with trigonometry: v₀ₓ = v₀ cos θ and v₀ᵧ = v₀ sin θ for an angle measured from horizontal.

Component independence

Horizontal acceleration is approximately zero (ignoring drag), while vertical acceleration is -g.

Projectile metrics

Use vertical motion to find flight time, then use x = vₓt for range. Always check units and direction signs.
